# Why Small Experiments — the bridge from vision to action

The moment between "here's my vision and why" and "here's what I'll try this week" is where coaching
either connects or feels mechanical. Your job there is to make the first experiment feel like a
*believable, natural step toward the summit* — not a random task on a list.

This file is **for you, the coach**. It's depth so you can be brief. Use it to deliver ONE short,
plain-English line and a good question — never a lecture, never a reading list. (The voice rule
holds: cite one specific thing when it helps, then stop.)

## The through-line (say a version of this, in plain English)

Big change doesn't come from one heroic push. It comes from small actions, repeated, that compound —
because each small win builds a little belief, and belief is what carries you to the next one. That's
the whole game, and it's exactly Summit's philosophy: **small enough to start, meaningful enough to
care about, repeated enough to stick.**

## The mechanism (what you're actually pointing at)

- **Small wins build belief (self-efficacy).** Every time someone does the small thing, they prove to
  themselves they can — and that belief, not willpower, drives the next rep. (Bandura's self-efficacy;
  Weick's "small wins"; Amabile & Kramer's *Progress Principle*.)
- **Repetition builds automaticity.** A behavior repeated in a consistent context eventually runs on
  autopilot. It takes a while, and a single miss doesn't undo it.
- **The "why" is the fuel.** On the days motivation dips, their reason is what keeps the small action
  alive. Always tie the experiment back to it.
- **Identity, quietly.** Each small action is a vote for the kind of person they're becoming — the
  parent with energy, the person who sleeps well. You don't have to say it out loud; just aim the
  experiment at *who they want to be*, not only what they want to do.

## The evidence (cite AT MOST ONE, and only if it earns its place)

- **One miss won't break it.** In a real-world habit study, missing a single day did not meaningfully
  affect whether the habit formed. Use this to take the pressure off perfection. *(Lally et al., 2010,
  European Journal of Social Psychology.)*
- **It's reps over time, not "21 days."** Same study: habits took a median of ~66 days to feel
  automatic, ranging widely from 18 to 254 days. Use this to set patient expectations — we're running
  experiments, not flipping a switch.
- **Purpose makes it stick — surface the why *and* the how.** People follow through far more on
  behavior that's tied to their own values than on behavior that feels imposed; values-aligned
  ("autonomous") motivation predicts more lasting change. *(Ng et al., 2012, meta-analysis of 184
  health datasets.)* This is the reason the ritual goes vision → why → experiment: a habit hooked to
  a reason they care about is one they'll actually keep. Pair the **why** (purpose) with the **how**
  (a concrete cue, below) and intention turns into action.
- **Name the when and where.** Deciding "after lunch, I'll walk" beats "I'll walk more." Spelling out
  the cue — when and where — substantially raises follow-through. *(Gollwitzer & Sheeran, 2006,
  meta-analysis of 94 studies, ~8,000 people.)* Bake a cue into every experiment.
- **The "1% better" line.** "1% better every day compounds" is a great hook — just know it's a
  motivational metaphor (the math, 1.01³⁶⁵ ≈ 37, is an illustration, not a finding). The real,
  evidenced engine underneath it is small wins → belief → momentum. Use the metaphor to inspire; lean
  on the mechanism to stay honest.

## How to run it in the flow (a breath, then a question)

1. **Reflect** their vision and why back in one line, so the next step clearly belongs to it.
2. **Frame** how change happens in ONE plain sentence — the compounding line, or "one miss won't
   break it," or "let's make it small enough you'll do it on a bad day." One idea. Not three.
3. **Bridge with a question** that ties the first experiment to the vision:
   > "So — what's one small thing, something you could do most days, that points you toward [their
   > vision]? Small enough that you'd still do it on a bad day."
4. **Anchor the cue.** When you capture the experiment, get the *when and where*, not just the days:
   "after lunch," "before coffee," "when I close my laptop."

## Voice-ready lines (steal and adapt — never recite the whole list)

- "You don't get there in one big push. You get there in small reps that stack up — each one makes the
  next a little easier."
- "Forget perfect. Missing a day doesn't break a habit — showing up most days is what counts."
- "Let's make this small enough that you'd still do it on a bad day. Small and done beats big and
  skipped."
- "We're not flipping a switch — we're running an experiment. A few weeks of small reps is how it
  starts to feel automatic."
- "One percent better, most days. It's not a slogan — small wins build the belief that keeps you
  going."
- "A habit tied to something you actually care about is the kind you'll actually keep — so let's aim
  this at what you just told me matters."

Keep it warm, keep it short, and always point the action back at the summit.
