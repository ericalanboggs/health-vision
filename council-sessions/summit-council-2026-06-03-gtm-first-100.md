# Summit Council — Go-to-Market to First 100 Users — June 3, 2026

## The question
> Is partnerships-first the right primary bet for fastest-to-100, or founder-optimism about a slow channel? Meta vs Google — is Meta-first right? Is running paid at all premature at ~18 users? What's the single highest-velocity path to 100 we're underweighting? Pre-mortem the plan.
>
> Proposed strategy: (1) Partnerships as the primary engine (practitioners/coaches by persona — lifestyle/cholesterol → primary-care, functional-med, GLP-1 telehealth; postpartum → pelvic-floor PT, OB/midwife, doulas; burnout → therapists, exec coaches, EAPs; + coaches as white-label/evangelist). (2) Paid as a small measured experiment ($300–500/mo cap), Meta first, small Google Search on high-intent "Noom alternative"/condition terms, judged on cost-per-trial via acquisition_source, don't scale until 3-mo retention clears 60%. (3) Organic: stop grinding IG; borrow audiences (podcasts, LinkedIn, Reddit) + SEO/condition content + lead magnets → email → lite challenge → trial. Context: ~18 users, 8-person pilot (3.9/5, median WTP ~$13), Core $12/Plus $49/Premium $119, blended LTV ~$706, ARPU ~$30, CAC <$30 organic/<$60 paid, 30% trial→paid. SMS+web, AI+human, vision-first, multi-behavior, not weight loss. Solo Mayo-certified-coach founder. Just shipped segment-aware onboarding + acquisition_source attribution. Product ships a 30-day trial vs plan's 7-day.

## Marcus (Operator)
Stop — eighteen users and we're three paragraphs into an acquisition conversation. That's backwards. Every dollar and partner intro spent on top-of-funnel is wasted if the bucket leaks, and you haven't shown me a month-3 retention number. At $30 ARPU and 8% monthly Core churn, Core LTV is thin and fragile; partnerships only pay if those warm users compound past month three.

Partnerships-first I'll allow — not because it's fast, but because it's cheap and reversible; you're not torching budget to learn. But three channels at once from a solo seat? No. Channel concentration wins at your stage. Founders who spread across paid + partnerships + content go an inch deep on each and learn nothing conclusive.

And kill the 30-day trial — it's hiding your single most important number. Fourteen days, maybe seven. You can't manage what you've buried under a month of "still trialing."

**He wants you to answer:** What is month-1 and month-3 retention on the current 18 — and why are we discussing acquisition before that number is good?

## Dr. Priya (Behavioral Scientist)
I'll defend partnerships-first for a reason your plan didn't state. A clinician or coach who *prescribes* Summit introduces an authority cue and a commitment device that cold advertising structurally cannot. The user's frame shifts from "another app I'll abandon" to "something my provider asked me to do." That reframe is real behavioral work — the difference between intent and adherence. This isn't just optimism; there's a mechanism.

But you're measuring the wrong end. Signups, clicks, trials are *intent*. What's the smallest habit a new user completes in week one, and does it survive to day thirty? Until I see that, every channel debate is premature — you might just be getting better at filling a funnel with people who feel inspired and do nothing.

Paid worries me precisely here: emotional landing pages optimize for the click feeling. You'll buy high-intent, low-follow-through users — the worst kind, because they convert and then ghost, indistinguishable from good users until the cohort ages.

**She wants you to answer:** What's the smallest week-1 behavior, and what's your day-7 and day-30 completion rate — split by warm (partner) vs cold (ad) source?

## Dr. James (Lifestyle Medicine Physician)
Two partnership lanes light up my caution: GLP-1 telehealth and cholesterol/primary-care. The moment a clinician's name sits next to "lower your cholesterol," that copy stops reading as wellness coaching and becomes a clinical claim. The AI guardrail is good, but co-branding amplifies claims risk an anonymous app doesn't carry. A partner stakes their license on you — they need it in writing that you're adjunct lifestyle support, not treatment, not a substitute for their care.

The failure I've watched for twenty years: a user feels "in control" because an app coaches their habits, and quietly deprioritizes the medication or follow-up that's actually moving the number. With GLP-1 and lipid patients, that's not hypothetical.

Burnout and postpartum lanes are lower-risk — coach away. It's the metabolic/cardiac-adjacent partnerships where I'd get the language airtight first.

**He wants you to answer:** When a referred patient's number doesn't move — or they skip a med because your habits made them feel handled — what's your written disclaimer, and has the partner signed off on it?

## Sasha (Growth & Distribution)
Half right, half too tidy. You nailed Meta-over-Google — Google Search for "SMS habit coaching" is a desert; you'd bleed budget onto competitive condition terms. For identity-based personas, Meta's the only lever, and the $300–500 cap with acquisition_source attribution is the discipline I'd want.

But "partnerships as *the engine* to 100 fastest" is where founders fool themselves. Partnerships are a relationship sales motion — durable and high-leverage, but slow and unforecastable. Half your enthusiastic partners ghost; onboarding one takes weeks; you can't will "3–5 partners" onto a timeline. The fastest early spike usually comes from a concentrated burst — one high-fit podcast, a sharp Product Hunt run, or the single partner sitting on a big list.

So my reframe: don't run paid to *acquire* — run it to *learn the hook*. Cheap Meta is the fastest, most honest message-testing lab you have. Find which persona and line converts, then walk into every partnership pitch and content piece carrying proof. Paid as R&D for the partnership pitch. And Meta-vs-Google is a rounding error next to creative — the hook is 80% of the outcome.

**She wants you to answer:** What's your single best hook, and have you tested it cheaply enough to *know* it converts before you ask a partner to stake their name on it?

## Dan (Skeptical Target User)
Honestly? A wellness ad on Instagram — I'd scroll past it without my brain registering it happened. I've got antibodies. Noom, Whoop, Headspace, the dusty Peloton — quit all of it, and each started with an ad or a free trial I forgot I signed up for. Cold Meta getting *me* to 100? Not your guy.

Flip it: if a coach I already pay, or my doctor, said "try this, it's just texts, not another app," I'd actually consider it. That's the only version that gets past the filter. So whatever the decks say, the warm/referral path is the one that moves a real person like me.

Two more gut checks. The $1 five-day text challenge — sure, *if a friend sends it*, not from a stranger's ad. And the 30-day trial works against you: give me a month and I'll forget I'm in it by week two. Give me a reason to show up *this Tuesday at 9pm*, not a runway to procrastinate.

**He wants you to answer:** Who that I already trust is going to put this in front of me — because the cold ad is not getting through, full stop?

## The Pre-Mortem Specialist
It's December 2027. Summit is dark. Eric, solo, ran at all three channels at once and got an inch deep in each. Partner meetings went great; almost none referred anyone, because there was no dead-simple mechanism and no incentive that survived their next busy week. The Meta test pulled clicks and a trickle of trials, so it stayed on — $400/mo, "still gathering data" — and the 30-day trial masked for months that almost nobody converted. The whole time, the original eighteen quietly churned, because retention was never the project; acquisition was. The bucket leaked faster than any channel could fill it.

The assumption that had to be wrong: that the problem was *awareness*. It wasn't. The product hadn't proven it retains, and every channel poured new people through a hole. The cruelest part — partnership goodwill got spent introducing warm, trusting users to a thing that didn't yet stick, so those advocates went quiet too.

**It wants you to answer:** If 3-month retention on your current 18 is under 60%, does any of this acquisition plan matter — and if not, why is acquisition what this whole conversation is about?

## Synthesis

**Where they aligned:**
- **Retention is the prior question.** Marcus, Priya, and the Pre-Mortem independently landed there: acquiring into an unproven-retention product is worse than not acquiring — it burns warm goodwill and budget on users who churn and tell people it didn't work. Convergence across three lenses = loudest signal in the room.
- **Kill the 30-day trial.** Marcus (hides the conversion number), Dan (invites procrastination), Sasha (slows learning), Pre-Mortem (masked failure for months) all hit it unprompted. Shorten to 7–14 days.
- **Warm > cold has a mechanism, not just a CAC story.** Dan (gut) and Priya (theory) converged: an authority/referral handoff is the only thing that gets past the target's ad-blindness. Partnerships-first survives — reframed as a behavioral advantage, not merely a cheap one.

**Where they fought:**
- **Paid: premature distraction or cheap learning lab?** Marcus + Pre-Mortem want zero acquisition spend until retention is proven and one channel chosen. Sasha wants a little paid now — but to learn the hook, not acquire. Reconciliation: paid as message R&D, capped and time-boxed, feeding the partnership pitch. Both reject "paid as a parallel acquisition engine."
- **Cholesterol/GLP-1 lane: highest-intent or highest-risk?** Sasha + Dan see it as the sharpest wedge; James wants to slow that exact lane on claims liability. Both right — most powerful and most dangerous, so airtight language *before* outreach.
- **Is partnerships-first fast?** Sasha's dissent: durable but unforecastable, so don't bank speed on it. Marcus: "fast" is the wrong goal at 18 users anyway.

**Surfaced blind spot:**
The question was framed as an *acquisition* problem ("fastest to 100"), but the council's strongest convergence is that you may not have earned the right to ask it — you don't know if the 18 retain. Reframe: the fastest *durable* path to 100 is to first prove retention on the people you have and fix the trial, then turn on partnerships — which double as your highest-signal retention test (warm users are more forgiving *and* more vocal). Second-order, from Sasha: use a tiny paid test not to acquire but to discover which persona-message converts, so you walk into partnership pitches carrying proof. Paid = R&D for the warm channel, not a competitor to it.

One line: *don't pour channels into a bucket you haven't pressure-tested. Prove the 18 stick, shorten the trial, then let partnerships be the engine — with cheap paid as the lab that sharpens the pitch.*
