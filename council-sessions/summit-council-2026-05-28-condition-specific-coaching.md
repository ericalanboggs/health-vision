# Summit Council — Condition-Specific Coaching — May 28, 2026

## The question
> Given the evidence that condition coaching = clinical protocol + MI (motivational interviewing), how — if at all — should Summit bake condition-specific coaching (smoking/vaping, diabetes, hypertension, weight loss, stress) into the product right now?

**Context the council was given:** ~20 users, pre-PMF, SMS + gpt-4o-mini AI coach, founder-led, subscription model. Summit already runs condition-adjacent challenges (healthy-hearts ≈ hypertension, stress-free ≈ stress). Stated differentiator: the "ask why" / MI conversational layer. Research finding: every clinical framework pairs a structural backbone (5 A's, ADCES7, DASH, DPP, MBSR) with MI as the conversational layer, and self-monitoring is the connective tissue.

---

## Marcus (Operator)
At 20 users you have no business building five condition verticals. The math: every condition you "support" is a content surface, a positioning promise, and a support burden, and you can't fill a single cohort yet. Condition-specific coaching is a post-PMF expansion move — it's how you lift ARPU and cut churn once you *know* people stick. You don't know that yet.

That said, there's a cheap version that actually helps the number I care about. Relevance is the #1 driver of month-3 retention in habit apps, and "the coach knows my specific thing" is pure relevance. So: don't build "the diabetes program." Make the coach smarter about the person in front of it. Zero new surface area, possible retention upside.

**He wants you to answer:** What does condition-specific work do to month-3 retention for the 20 users you already have — and can you name one of them it would have saved?

## Dr. Priya (Behavioral Scientist)
Your research got the architecture right: protocol + MI, self-monitoring as connective tissue. The trap is implementing the protocols *as features* — a "5 A's module," an "ADCES7 checklist." That's compliance theater. Look closer and the protocols share a tiny set of coachable behaviors: self-monitoring, trigger/antecedent mapping, problem-solving for high-risk situations, relapse-prevention planning (Wood's habit-cue work and Fogg's behavior model both sit underneath all five). Install *those* generically. Let the condition change the content, not the machinery.

What genuinely differs by condition is the target behavior and the feedback signal. A smoker maps cravings to triggers; a hypertensive self-monitors BP; a person with diabetes reads glucose patterns. Same behavior-change skeleton, different signal. Encode the skeleton once; teach the coach the per-condition signal.

**She wants you to answer:** What's the single smallest coachable behavior per condition, and how would you know it stuck — what's the signal in the *data*, not the self-report?

## Dr. James (Lifestyle Medicine Physician)
Split your five conditions in half right now, because they are not the same animal. Smoking/vaping, weight loss, and stress are wellness coaching — coach away. Diabetes and hypertension are *managed medical conditions*. The moment your AI coaches someone on "insulin titration confidence" or interprets their BP, you are one gpt-4o-mini hallucination away from harm and a claim you cannot defend at your stage.

You can absolutely support people who have these conditions — the framing is "we help you stick to the plan your doctor gave you," never "we manage your diabetes." Self-monitoring reminders and adherence nudges are safe. Interpreting numbers, suggesting medication changes, or implying clinical outcomes ("lower your A1c with Summit") is the line. Build the guardrail before you write a word of the content.

**He wants you to answer:** What's the worst-case user — the Type 1 diabetic who reads your AI's glucose "coaching" as medical advice — and what concretely stops your model from crossing that line?

## Sasha (Growth & Distribution)
Condition-specific is the best acquisition story you have. "Wellness habit app" is unsearchable and untargetable — you're bidding against Noom and Calm for a generic intent and losing. "Quit vaping," "lower blood pressure naturally," "habit coach for prediabetes" are high-intent, lower-competition searches that map to real, Meta-targetable audiences. Each condition is a landing page, a creative angle, a cohort.

But you don't need to *be* a condition company to *market* by condition. Pick ONE condition as the wedge — best intent-to-safety ratio — and make it the front door to the general product. Based on your own research, my vote is smoking/vaping: huge population that wants to quit, evidence base wide open (vaping cessation is barely studied), and zero medical-claim landmine.

**She wants you to answer:** If you had to pick one condition as your paid-acquisition wedge for the next 90 days, which one — and what's the CAC ceiling that makes it work?

## Dan (Skeptical Target User)
Honestly? "Condition-specific" cuts both ways for me. If Summit billed itself as "a habit app for stress," I'd glaze over — I already have Headspace lapsed on my phone. But if a friend said "it's the thing that actually got me off vaping," I'd look. The condition makes it concrete, and concrete beats wellness-soup every time.

The second it feels medical, though, I'm gone. If it starts quizzing me about my blood pressure numbers it feels like homework my doctor assigned, not a habit I'm building for myself. I came to build a habit, not to get managed. The win is when it feels like it *gets* my specific thing without turning into a clinic.

**He wants you to answer:** Is this the thing that helped me quit a specific thing, or another general wellness app wearing a lab coat? Because I'll try the first and ignore the second.

## The Pre-Mortem Specialist
It's late 2027. Summit is shut down. The likely story: Eric read a sharp research summary, got excited, and tried to support all five conditions while he still had 20 users. The product went a mile wide and an inch deep — five half-built condition tracks, none credible enough to win a category, and the general product never reached PMF because attention was scattered across cohorts that never filled. The runway burned on protocol modules nobody used.

The other death, less likely but fatal: Summit leaned into diabetes and hypertension, the AI said something a user treated as medical advice, and a wellness-stage company discovered it had no business carrying that liability. Either way, the thing that killed Summit was treating condition coaching as a 2026 *build* instead of a 2027 *earned expansion*.

**He wants you to answer:** Which kills you first — spreading across five conditions before PMF, or one medical-claim incident on the two clinical ones? Plan against the more likely one.

## Anjali (Brand Strategist)
General wellness owns no word. "Summit: your wellness journey" is wallpaper. The brands that broke through owned exactly one thing — Whoop owned recovery, Noom owned the psychology of eating. So the condition question is really: do you want a word? "The habit coach that asks why" is a behavior-word. "Quit vaping for good" is a condition-word. You can layer them — the condition is the door, the MI is the room behind it.

But do not claim five conditions. A brand that says it does everything says it does nothing. Pick the one condition whose user you understand best and that's truest to the "ask why" soul, and let the other four be quiet capabilities, not headlines.

**She wants you to answer:** What's the one word or outcome you want users to say you own — and does any single condition let you own it faster than "wellness" ever will?

## Synthesis

**Where they aligned (signals):**
- Near-unanimous: **do not build five condition programs now.** Marcus (focus), Priya (machinery, not modules), Pre-Mortem (mile wide), and Anjali (no brand owns five things) all converge independently. When the operator, the scientist, the risk specialist, and the brand strategist agree from four different directions, that's the strongest signal in the room.
- Strong alignment that the leverage is **condition-aware coaching *context* (the MI layer), not condition-specific protocol *features*.** Priya, Marcus, Dan, and Anjali all point at "make the coach understand my specific thing" over "ship a protocol module."

**Where they fought:**
- **Focus vs. Growth (Marcus vs. Sasha):** Marcus says no conditions until PMF; Sasha says one condition is your only real CAC unlock and you should pick the wedge *now*. Resolves by separating *marketing by condition* (cheap, Sasha's right, do it) from *building condition programs* (expensive, Marcus is right, wait). You can run a condition-specific acquisition wedge without building a condition-specific product.
- **Caution vs. Concreteness (James vs. Sasha/Dan):** James wants the two clinical conditions walled behind guardrails; Sasha and Dan note that conditions are precisely what make Summit legible and marketable. Resolves via the two-tier split below — market and coach the wellness conditions freely; for diabetes/hypertension, "support your care plan," never "manage your condition."

**Surfaced blind spot (highest-value output):**
You've been treating "condition coaching" as one decision across five conditions. It's two decisions, because **the five split into two tiers:**
- **Wellness tier** (smoking/vaping, weight loss, stress) — safe to coach and market now.
- **Clinical tier** (diabetes, hypertension) — managed medical conditions; "support the doctor's plan" framing + hard guardrails, or defer entirely.

And the thing to "bake in" is *not* protocol modules — it's **condition-aware context for the AI coach** so it asks the right "why" and targets the right high-leverage behavior per person (trigger-mapping for the smoker, sodium + BP self-monitoring for the hypertensive, food/weight self-monitoring for weight loss). That version is cheap, reversible, on-brand, and the only one every advisor could live with. The expensive, risky version — productized clinical-condition programs — is a 2027 problem to defer explicitly.

Bonus the council surfaced: you're *already* condition-adjacent (healthy-hearts ≈ hypertension, stress-free ≈ stress), so the real question was never "should I enter conditions." It's "how deep should the coaching layer go," and the answer is: as deep as a smarter prompt, not as deep as a new product.
