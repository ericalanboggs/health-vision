# Summit Council — Postpartum Landing Page Positioning — May 29, 2026

## The question
> Should the /postpartum landing page commit to postpartum/early-motherhood as the sharp hook (with "coming back to yourself" as the deeper promise), or broaden fully to "moms rebuilding and choosing themselves"? Eric leans toward the broader "just being a mom" framing.

**Panel:** Maya (postpartum user), Anjali (brand strategist), Sasha (growth) — focused consult, full format.
**Context:** Current draft straddles (broad "moms" headline, postpartum everything-else). Pre-PMF (~20 users). Only mom proof = AJ (postpartum). Sibling pages: knowledge-worker burnout, men-40+. Draft rejects "bounce back"; identity-recovery framed; 3-months-to-3-years range. Avoid medical scope.

## Maya (Postpartum User)
Real talk: "Coaching for moms rebuilding" — I scroll past that. It sounds like every ad my feed already serves me between the baby-food reels. "Rebuilding," "choosing yourself" — I want to believe it, but on a Tuesday it reads like a candle brand. What stops my thumb is proof they know *this* specific season: that I haven't had a predictable 30 minutes in eight months, that I don't recognize my own energy, that I love her and also I'm just... not in here right now. Name that and I'll read the next line.

Here's the knot, though. I'm six months out and "postpartum" still fits me. My friend who's two years out is living the exact same thing and would say "that's not me anymore." So the word "postpartum" is both what finds me and what loses her. I don't need the clinical badge stamped on every line — I need the lived thing said plainly. Lead with what it *feels* like, not what it's *called*.

And whatever you do: one "bounce back" or "your best self" and I'm gone. Not because I'm sensitive. Because it tells me you don't actually get it.

**She wants you to answer:** Does the page make me feel recognized in the specific season — or like one more mom you're selling "better" to?

## Anjali (Brand Strategist)
Eric, the instinct to broaden is the instinct that kills brands. "Moms rebuilding and choosing themselves" — name one word you own in that. You can't, because everyone owns it, so no one does. "Choose yourself" is the "live your best life" of 2026. It's wallpaper.

Postpartum is a word you can actually own, because the category serves that moment with shame or with a six-week ab program and almost nobody serves it with "come back to yourself, no bounce-back, no deadline." That's a gap with a name. You earn the right to "moms" by first being undeniably the thing for one moment — not by opening with a hug for everyone. Narrow is how you eventually get wide.

So hold the line: postpartum is the wedge. "Coming back to yourself" isn't your category — it's your promise, the through-line under everything. Own the moment, mean the promise.

**She wants you to answer:** What's the one sentence only Summit can say to a postpartum mom that a Peloton or a Noom literally couldn't say with a straight face?

## Sasha (Growth & Distribution)
Let me reframe the whole thing, because you're treating a landing page like a brand identity. It isn't. It's a conversion asset, and you already run three. "Postpartum vs. moms" isn't a fork you have to resolve philosophically — it's an A/B test you can just run.

That said, if I'm spending a dollar today: postpartum, every time. "Postpartum energy," "postpartum coaching that isn't bounce-back" — real searches, real intent, almost no good supply. I can target postpartum groups, OB waiting rooms, pelvic-floor PT referral partners, the fourth-trimester podcast world. "Moms rebuilding" is a $40 CPM into a wall: no intent, no targeting handle, competing with every mom-brand alive. Postpartum is a channel. "Moms" is a vibe.

One caution so I'm not just cheerleading: postpartum users age out, so this is a fantastic front door, not necessarily the whole house. Acquire on the sharp wedge, then let the broader "coming back to yourself" promise carry them as they leave the window.

**She wants you to answer:** Will you actually run this as a targeted acquisition page (search + postpartum communities + referral partners), or will it just sit at /postpartum hoping for traffic? That answer decides whether "narrow" is an asset or a ceiling.

## Synthesis

**Where they aligned:** All three reject the broad "moms choosing themselves" as the page's *hook* — Anjali (owns no word), Sasha (unsearchable, untargetable), Maya (scrolls right past it; sounds like a candle brand). That three-way agreement from brand, growth, and user is the strongest signal here: the broad framing is the weakest version of the page. All three endorse "coming back to yourself" as the *promise/meaning* — just not as the hook.

**Where they fought:**
- *Anjali vs. Sasha (altitude):* Anjali wants to OWN postpartum as the brand word for the long haul; Sasha says it's a conversion asset and a front door — acquire on it now, don't marry the brand to a window users age out of. Both right at different altitudes (brand vs. page).
- *Maya vs. Anjali (the literal word):* Anjali wants to own the word "postpartum"; Maya warns the clinical word both finds her and loses the two-years-out mom living the same life, and can feel like a badge. The resolution lives in separating targeting (use the word) from on-page voice (lead with the felt season).

**Surfaced blind spot:** You framed this as a brand-identity choice — "are we for postpartum moms or all moms?" But it's a landing page, and you run three. The architecture the council surfaced: **postpartum is THIS page's wedge** (the one with proof, search intent, and an ownable gap), and **"coming back to yourself" is the cross-funnel PROMISE** that already unifies all three pages — your brand spine, not this page's headline. So your instinct toward "moms rebuilding and choosing themselves" isn't wrong; it's misplaced — it's the umbrella meaning, not the postpartum hook. And Maya's quieter blind spot: separate the word that FINDS her (postpartum — title, meta, targeting) from the language that RESONATES (lived experience — the on-page voice), so the clinical label never does the emotional work and never boxes out the mom who's aged out of it.
