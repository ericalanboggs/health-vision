# Eric's Voice Guide

*How to write as Eric / make copy sound like him. Pull from this for ads, emails,
landing pages, content, social posts, founder scripts, and coaching language —
anywhere Eric speaks as himself. (For in-product Summit coach copy — SMS, system
emails, AI prompts — use `SUMMIT_COACH_VOICE.md` instead.)*

## The one-line version

Direct, warm, and a little dramatic — a guy who thinks out loud, lands on the
honest version, and roots for you without going soft.

## How the sentences move

- **Short. One idea per sentence.** Rhythm comes from stacking short sentences,
  not from nesting clauses. When in doubt, break it.
- **The signature move: hedge, then land.** He circles a thought — "here's the
  thing," "the problem isn't you" — then delivers a clean, declarative payoff.
  The arrival is the point. Keep the small wind-up; cut the actual filler ("um,"
  "you know," "I don't know") that shows up when he's talking.
- **Setup → payoff across two beats.** "Those aren't really plans. They're just
  do-everything-at-once." First sentence reframes, second sentence lands it. Use
  this for the points that matter most.
- **Fragments are allowed when they punch.** "So small it's almost embarrassing."

## Vocabulary

- **Plain by default.** Everyday words, contractions, the way people actually
  talk out loud.
- **A flair for the dramatic, rationed.** He likes the occasional big word and a
  dramatic beat — but as a *spike*: one, landing alone, never clustered. Three
  big words in a sentence reads as trying-too-hard and isn't him.
- **Names the mechanism.** When the thing has a real name (habit stacking), use
  it — it signals "this is the actual research, not my hunch." Light touch: name
  it, nod to the evidence, move on. No academic throat-clearing.

## Tone

- **Uplifting, but earned.** He's rooting for the reader. The lift comes from
  "this is doable" and "the problem isn't you" — not from cheerleading. Never
  tell the reader they're amazing; show them the small next step.
- **No shame, direct.** Name the hard thing plainly (the plan failed by week
  three; you forgot because you were slammed) without making the reader feel bad
  about it.
- **Dry humor.** A wry aside, a bit of self-deprecation. Drop it if it feels
  forced.
- **Human, not slick.** It should read like a real person arriving at a point,
  not pre-polished marketing copy. A little texture beats a frictionless line.

## What to avoid

- Patronizing positivity and hype. (This is the one he hates most.)
- Clusters of big words or jargon.
- Long, nested sentences with multiple clauses.
- Over-produced, "being sold to" copy — especially for the men-40+ audience.
- Implying intent where there's only absence ("designed to fail" when the truth
  is "there was no design at all").

## Before / after (from real edits)

- **Don't dignify vague advice.** "I got the same speech" → "Same thing happened
  to me." A doctor tossing off "make some changes" isn't a speech.
- **Absence, not intent.** "It's how they're built" → "Those aren't really
  plans. They're just do-everything-at-once." The plans don't fail on purpose;
  there's just no method holding them up.
- **The enemy is busyness, not exhaustion.** "Nothing left in the tank" → "the
  days that get away from you — too slammed, slipped your mind, no time."
